Black Eco Feminisms

A geospatial storytelling atlas for Black environmental justice movements

Black Eco Feminisms is an interactive atlas and oral history archive that reveals how Black women-led coalitions respond to climate stressors. I led data storytelling, integrating satellite imagery, sensor feeds, and community interviews into actionable visual narratives for policy advocates.

Role Data Storytelling Lead

Designed the geospatial architecture and facilitated participatory mapping sessions.

Stack PostGIS · Deck.gl · Kepler.gl · Airflow · PostgreSQL

Nightly pipelines harmonizing Landsat imagery, EPA pollution data, and community-collected air quality metrics.

Community Partners 6 grassroots organizations

Including West Oakland Environmental Indicators Project and the Black Ecology Center.

Launch Beta released Nov 2023

Presented at the Environmental Justice Now Summit with 300+ attendees.

Project context

Environmental justice conversations are often dominated by metrics devoid of lived experience. Black women organizers asked us for a tool that preserves narrative agency while equipping them with credible evidence for grant proposals and council meetings.

Core themes

  • Interdependence. Visualizing how housing, health, and ecology intersect block-by-block.
  • Reparative storytelling. Pairing satellite change detection with oral histories to highlight resilience strategies.
  • Self-determination. Providing communities direct control over data redaction and access permissions.

Research & findings

We co-designed qualitative coding rubrics with organizers, then developed a Deck.gl layer system that overlays particulate matter spikes with interview excerpts. Policy teams used the platform to argue for funding targeted tree canopies and respiratory clinics.

Change detection Participatory GIS Narrative analytics Accessibility-first UX

Impact & contributions

  • Secured three municipal grants totaling $1.8M for community-led climate mitigation projects.
  • Adopted by two journalism collaboratives to contextualize investigative reporting on heat islands.
  • Published an open methodology guide downloaded 2,400+ times in the first quarter.

Lessons forward

  • Building a federated publishing workflow so partner organizations can host localized versions offline.
  • Expanding to include audio narratives with Web Captioner transcripts for accessibility.
  • Partnering with public schools to integrate the atlas into environmental science curricula.

Land Back with Conditions: Ninth Root Internship

Environmental Justice Urban Planning Civic Design

As part of my UC Berkeley summer fieldwork and Arcus Social Justice Fellowship, I completed an Urban Planning Internship with Ninth Root, an Oakland-based environmental justice nonprofit. I worked alongside community organizers and residents to design listening sessions that addressed land use, displacement, and ecological care. This project investigated how civic design tools—such as systems mapping, co-creation workshops, and storytelling—could strengthen community agency in environmental planning and stewardship.

Key contributions

  • Community Listening Sessions: Designed and facilitated sessions centering intergenerational narratives around environmental harm, healing, and land justice.
  • Civic Design Strategy: Mapped relationships between community assets, governance structures, and environmental inequities to inform participatory planning.
  • Workshop Design: Co-created creative engagements that merged art, policy dialogue, and local activism to translate residents’ stories into actionable frameworks.
  • Training & Visual Tools: Produced volunteer recruitment materials, facilitation guides, and visual systems maps to support Ninth Root’s ongoing initiatives.

Ongoing scholarship

This fieldwork informs my forthcoming chapter on Black Ecofeminisms and Land Stewardship in Indigenous Sovereignty in Environmental Research: Decolonizing Ethics and Methodologies (Routledge, forthcoming). The chapter extends this applied design work, positioning stewardship as both a civic design practice and a framework of care within environmental and technological systems.

Impact

Turned community listening into civic intelligence—translating lived experiences into design artifacts that guide equitable environmental planning and future coalition-building.
